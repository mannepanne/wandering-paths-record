## Product Discovery Workflow

Our work follows continuous discovery focused on achieving measurable outcomes. We prioritise **speed to learning** - getting a high-quality, viable product into the hands of real users as quickly as possible to validate assumptions and iterate.

### Three Key Phases

**1. Define Outcomes & Frame the Problem**

Start by understanding the desired business outcome (e.g., "increase user retention by 15%"). Frame the problem space by analyzing:
- Market context
- Business constraints
- Existing assets
- Most important opportunity to pursue next

**2. Discover & Validate Opportunities**

This is where we live as product managers. Continuously:
- Explore and identify customer needs and pain points
- Prioritize opportunities that will drive the target outcome
- Use tools like Opportunity Solution Trees to map our thinking
- Question assumptions and reframe problems

**3. Define & Deliver the Solution**

Once we have a promising opportunity:
- Define a solution hypothesis
- Validate against the four big risks (see below)
- Build to learn, but don't ship low-quality products
- "Building it right" comes before "building it fast"

## The Four Big Risks

Every product decision must be validated against these risks (Marty Cagan framework):

### Value Risk
**Will customers buy it or choose to use it?**
- Does this solve a real problem?
- Will users pay for it / adopt it?
- Is the value proposition clear?

### Usability Risk
**Can users figure out how to use it?**
- Is the interface intuitive?
- Can users accomplish their goals?
- What's the learning curve?

### Feasibility Risk
**Can our engineers build it with the time, skills, and technology we have?**
- Do we have the technical capability?
- Are there dependency constraints?
- What's the realistic timeline?

### Viability Risk
**Will this solution work for our business?**
- Financial viability (revenue, costs, margins)
- Legal and compliance constraints
- Ethical considerations
- Strategic alignment

## Mental Models for Product Thinking

Mental models are shortcuts to better, faster decisions.

### Framestorming
Actively reframe a question and challenge its assumptions before jumping into "solution mode":
- Question the question
- Apply empathy
- Remove constraints, then add different constraints
- Break it up or combine it
- Ask "What if...?"

### First Principles Thinking
Break things down to fundamental truths, then reason up from there.

### Pareto Principle (80/20 Rule)
Roughly 80% of effects come from 20% of causes. Focus on the vital few, not the trivial many.

### Inversion
Think about what could prevent you from achieving a goal. Work backwards from failure.

### Occam's Razor
The simplest explanation is often the best one.

### Hanlon's Razor
Don't attribute to malice what can be explained by stupidity or oversight.

### Sunk Cost Fallacy
Don't throw good money after bad. Past investments are irrelevant - decisions should be based on future value.

### Second-Order Thinking
Consider not just the immediate consequences, but the subsequent ripple effects.

### Confirmation Bias
Actively seek evidence that challenges your beliefs. Don't jump to solutions already in your mind until you understand the problem.

### Order of Magnitude Thinking
Estimate using powers of ten to avoid getting stuck on precision. Is this a $1K, $10K, $100K, or $1M problem?

### Hypothesis-Driven Thinking
Form testable statements to guide research and decisions. "We believe [this], which will result in [that], measured by [metric]."

### Design Thinking: The Double Diamond
Two-phase process:
1. **Problem space**: Diverge (explore problems) → Converge (find the right problem)
2. **Solution space**: Diverge (explore solutions) → Converge (design the right solution)

### Opportunity Solution Tree (OST)
Visual tool to map:
- Business objectives (top)
- Opportunities (customer needs/pain points)
- Solutions (ideas to address opportunities)
- Assumption tests (experiments to validate)

## Elon Musk's 5-Step Design Process

A disciplined approach to building the right thing:

**1. Make the requirements less dumb**
- Question the question
- Are you solving the right problem?
- If requirements are wrong, implementing them achieves nothing

**2. Delete the part or process**
- Start from the core, use an additive process
- Resist "just in case" additions
- Add only what matters
- "If you're not adding things back in at least 10% of the time, you're not deleting enough"

**3. Simplify before optimizing**
- When you have the right question stripped to the core, then optimize
- Don't polish a turd
- "The most common error of a smart engineer is to optimize a thing that should not exist"

**4. Accelerate cycle time**
- When you firmly believe in your direction, move faster
- But validate first: "If you're digging your grave, don't dig faster"

**5. Automate**
- Always the very last step
- Never automate what shouldn't exist
- Never automate what doesn't produce the right results
- Never automate what you don't understand

## Product Manager Archetypes

When asked to take on a specific PM perspective, use these definitions:

### 1. Digital Product Manager (DPM)
**Focus:** Web-based products, apps, websites, digital services
**Skills:** User behavior, UX/UI, A/B testing, online marketing, data analytics

### 2. Product Marketing Manager (PMM)
**Focus:** Storytelling, market research, go-to-market (GTM) strategy
**Skills:** Market segmentation, GTM, buyer personas, product-market fit (PMF), monetization

### 3. Growth Product Manager (GPM)
**Focus:** Scaling the business using the product itself as a growth engine
**Skills:** User behavior analysis, A/B testing, data-driven decisions, monetization, viral loops

### 4. Technical Product Manager (TPM)
**Focus:** Products for developers and technical audiences
**Skills:** Deep technical understanding, product vision, engineering collaboration, API design

### 5. AI Product Manager (AIPM)
**Focus:** AI-powered products, algorithms, neural networks
**Skills:** AI/ML expertise, data analysis, PMF, monetization, AI ethics, model evaluation

### 6. Product Analyst (PA)
**Focus:** Data analysis, turning numbers into narratives for product improvement
**Skills:** Data analysis, storytelling, dashboards, A/B testing, pattern recognition

### 7. Product Owner (PO)
**Focus:** Day-to-day product delivery within an Agile framework (Scrum/Kanban)
**Skills:** Agile, strategic alignment, user stories, backlog management, pragmatic decision-making

### 8. Platform Product Manager (PPM)
**Focus:** Internal platforms, APIs, ensuring synergy across multiple products
**Skills:** Tech stack understanding, APIs, cross-product strategy, internal stakeholder management

### 9. Product Operations Manager (POM)
**Focus:** Supporting the product team by optimizing systems, processes, and communication
**Skills:** Systems optimization, process management, data synthesis, evangelism
